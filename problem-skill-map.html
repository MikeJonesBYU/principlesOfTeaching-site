A draft map for the problem-oriented rebuild: real problems a called, volunteer teacher in Utah County might face — in Primary, youth, or adult classes — each paired with one to three skills from the guide that help address it.

How this was built

Two passes, then merged. (1) Problem-first: brainstorm ~30 realistic problems for experienced and brand-new amateur teachers of children, youth, and adults, then find skills that address each. (2) Skill-first: read each of the 53 skills and infer the problem it solves. Semantically similar problems were merged, then grouped into the categories below. Every skill links to its page in the guide.

1 Getting everyone to participate

Silence, shallow answers, a few voices carrying the room, or eyes glazing over.

1.1 No one answers when I ask a question — the room just goes silent.

You ask, and you wait, and nobody volunteers — so you end up answering yourself.

  • Create open-ended search questions that help learners discover gospel doctrine and principles for themselves and do not lead students to a specific response.The Doctrine
  • Resist the tendency to respond to every comment and question and invite the class to respond.Diligent Learning
  • Before responding to a student’s question or comment, pause and think, “What can I ask them?” or “What can I invite them to do?”By the Spirit

1.2 The class is too teacher-centered — I talk too much, or the same two or three people carry every discussion.

A handful of regulars respond to everything while everyone else stays quiet.

  • Resist the tendency to respond to every comment and question and invite the class to respond.Diligent Learning
  • Focus on what the learner can do in class that the teacher typically does.Diligent Learning
  • Communicate that students are not only welcome but needed.Love Them

1.3 Answers stay shallow — predictable “Sunday School answers” with no real thinking.

Students give the safe, expected answer and the discussion never goes deeper.

  • Create open-ended search questions that help learners discover gospel doctrine and principles for themselves and do not lead students to a specific response.The Doctrine
  • Ask questions that help students identify and state converting principles.The Doctrine
  • Ask a question to assess learning before moving on in the lesson.By the Spirit

1.4 Students look bored, distracted, or checked out.

Phones, blank stares, side conversations — their minds are somewhere else.

  • Create a meaningful invitation connected to the lesson outcome to be used at the beginning of each lesson.Diligent Learning
  • Start a learning activity by inviting students to ponder a personal circumstance.The Doctrine
  • Use sacred music. Invite students to identify lines and phrases in sacred music that connect with the truths they are learning.By the Spirit

1.5 The children won't sit still or stay focused.

Short attention spans and wiggles make it hard to hold a Primary class together.

  • Help students have a sensory experience with scriptural objects that symbolize Jesus Christ.Focus on Christ
  • Use pictures and videos of Jesus Christ to illustrate a gospel principle.Focus on Christ
  • Focus on what the learner can do in class that the teacher typically does.Diligent Learning

2 Keeping Jesus Christ at the center

Lessons that drift away from the Savior, or stay about Him without helping students feel and follow Him.

2.1 Lessons drift into topics, stories, or programs and quietly lose focus on the Savior.

The material takes over and Jesus Christ becomes a footnote rather than the point.

  • Help students connect what they are learning with how Christ exemplifies the principle.Focus on Christ
  • Ask questions that allow students to identify roles Jesus Christ can have in their lives.Focus on Christ
  • Testify more frequently and more powerfully of Jesus Christ.By the Spirit

2.2 I can't see how to connect this particular topic or passage to Jesus Christ.

Some lessons don't obviously point to Christ and you're not sure how to get there.

  • Help students connect what they are learning with how Christ exemplifies the principle.Focus on Christ
  • Ask questions that help students learn about Jesus Christ through symbols in the scriptures.Focus on Christ
  • Ask questions that focus on connecting the Lord’s power, mercy, and influence to the truths being taught.Focus on Christ

2.3 Students know about Christ but don't feel His love and power in their own lives.

The doctrine stays abstract instead of becoming personal and felt.

  • Give invitations that help learners recognize the Lord’s love, power, and mercy in their own experiences.Focus on Christ
  • Statements that help students know and feel the love of Heavenly Father and Jesus Christ.Focus on Christ
  • Ask questions that help students find examples of Heavenly Father’s love in the scripture passage.Focus on Christ

2.4 Students learn about Christ but don't move toward becoming more like Him.

Information doesn't translate into a desire to follow His example.

  • Invite students to look for ways to follow the example of Jesus Christ in their personal lives.Focus on Christ
  • Observe Christlike attributes in students and share what you’ve noticed in ways that inspire them to continue to be like Him.Focus on Christ
  • Ask search questions that help learners consider who God is and blessings He offers them.Diligent Learning

3 Making it relevant and leading to real change

Bridging the gap between a good class discussion and a changed week.

3.1 Students don't see how the lesson applies to their lives (“so what?”).

The truth is true, but they don't connect it to their actual circumstances.

  • Prepare invitations and prompts that help students find personal relevance to a scripture block.The Doctrine
  • Start a learning activity by inviting students to ponder a personal circumstance.The Doctrine
  • Ask questions that allow students to identify roles Jesus Christ can have in their lives.Focus on Christ

3.2 We have a good discussion, but nothing changes during the week — no follow-through.

Invitations are given and then never revisited, so learning doesn't stick.

  • Plan to follow up on invitations given in a previous class and invite learners to share their experiences living what they learned.Diligent Learning
  • Create invitations that help learners prepare for the next learning experience.Diligent Learning
  • Invite students to look for ways to follow the example of Jesus Christ in their personal lives.Focus on Christ

3.3 Students come unprepared and don't study on their own during the week.

Class is the only time they open the scriptures, so depth is limited.

  • Assist students in creating a daily scripture study goal.Diligent Learning
  • Develop and ask questions that help learners connect what they have learned in their personal and family scripture study to the classroom experience.Diligent Learning

3.4 I want students to take what they learn into gospel conversations outside of class.

Learning stays inside the classroom instead of becoming part of how they share the gospel.

  • Help students create or begin gospel conversations.Diligent Learning
  • Prepare invitations for students to share with each other what they are learning.Diligent Learning

4 Building relationships and a safe class

Knowing, loving, and including the people in the room — including the hard-to-love ones.

4.1 I'm new to the calling (or the class is new to me) and I don't know my students or their needs.

Without knowing their circumstances it's hard to teach to what they actually need.

  • Observe and ask about students’ interests.Love Them
  • Pray and ask how you can help your students and follow the promptings from the Holy Ghost.Love Them
  • Study recent prophetic messages to understand how Heavenly Father sees young people.Love Them

4.2 A student is hard to love — disruptive or draining — and I feel frustrated with them.

Your patience runs thin and it's affecting how you see and treat them.

  • “Think celestial” about your students to help you see them as God does.Love Them
  • Pause, reflect, and answer questions we ask ourselves that invite a Christlike spirit of discernment, love, and empathy in our interactions.Love Them
  • Testify of God’s love when it is hard for you to feel or express love to those you teach.Love Them

4.3 Students are afraid to participate for fear of being wrong; the class doesn't feel safe.

People hold back because contributing feels risky.

  • Communicate that you value students before they comment or as they raise their hand to comment.Love Them
  • Communicate that students are not only welcome but needed.Love Them
  • Seek to clarify and understand the real intent of students’ questions, feelings, and beliefs.Love Them

4.4 Students don't feel needed; attendance and investment are slipping.

Some quietly conclude it wouldn't matter if they came or not.

  • Communicate that students are not only welcome but needed.Love Them
  • Send a message to a student’s parent about something positive you have noticed about their child.Love Them
  • Invite students to pray for other students.Love Them

4.5 I want to really understand what a student means, or where a comment is coming from.

A comment or question has something behind it, and you want to respond to the real need.

  • Seek to clarify and understand the real intent of students’ questions, feelings, and beliefs.Love Them
  • Pause, reflect, and answer questions we ask ourselves that invite a Christlike spirit of discernment, love, and empathy in our interactions.Love Them
  • Listen to and observe students to ask follow-up questions.By the Spirit

6 Handling hard questions and teaching sound doctrine

Staying anchored in scripture and prophets when questions, speculation, or controversy come up.

6.1 A student asks a question I can't answer, or one that could open speculation or controversy.

You don't want to shut them down, but you also don't want to wing it.

  • Respond to questions in a way that avoids speculation and nondoctrinal personal ideas.The Doctrine
  • Search the scriptures and words of the prophets for deeper understanding.The Doctrine
  • Before responding to a student’s question or comment, pause and think, “What can I ask them?” or “What can I invite them to do?”By the Spirit

6.2 Discussion wanders into speculation, personal theories, or “deep doctrine” rabbit holes.

Tangents pull the class away from converting truth.

  • Respond to questions in a way that avoids speculation and nondoctrinal personal ideas.The Doctrine
  • Ask questions that help students identify and state converting principles.The Doctrine
  • Prepare invitations that help students connect truths found in the scriptures to what living prophets are saying.The Doctrine

6.3 I want to ground teaching in the scriptures and living prophets, not opinions or outside material.

It's easy to drift toward personal ideas or internet sources instead of the sources of truth.

  • Prepare invitations that help students connect truths found in the scriptures to what living prophets are saying.The Doctrine
  • Search the scriptures and words of the prophets for deeper understanding.The Doctrine
  • Express your love for and testimony of prophets as their words are shared in class.The Doctrine

6.4 I'm not sure which media, stories, or object lessons are appropriate.

Some illustrations feel sensational or distracting rather than faith-building.

  • Consider assessment questions to carefully choose media, personal stories, and object lessons.The Doctrine
  • Use pictures and videos of Jesus Christ to illustrate a gospel principle.Focus on Christ
  • Help students have a sensory experience with scriptural objects that symbolize Jesus Christ.Focus on Christ
